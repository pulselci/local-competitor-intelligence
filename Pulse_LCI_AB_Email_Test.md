# Pulse LCI — A/B Cold Email Test Framework

## The Experiment

**What we know:**
- ~50% open rate → subject lines are working
- Near-zero website visits → the email body isn't converting to clicks
- Zero free report requests → something is breaking between open and action

**What we're diagnosing:**
- Group A tests whether the *website* is the barrier (open → click → website → form = 4 steps)
- Group B tests whether the *ask itself* is the barrier (open → reply = 2 steps)

**The funnel hypothesis:**
If Group B gets significantly more replies → the website/form is the friction point, not the value proposition.  
If both underperform → the value proposition itself isn't resonating, and we have a deeper messaging problem.  
If Group A gets clicks but no form fills → the landing page is the problem.

---

## GROUP A — Self-Serve Model

**Goal:** Drive to website to request a free report  
**Variable being tested:** Can a clear curiosity hook + strong CTA overcome website friction?  
**Psychology:** Competitive threat + curiosity gap. The prospect feels like they're missing information their competitors might already have. The website visit feels like *their* decision, not a sales interaction.

---

### Group A — Subject Lines (pick one per send)

| # | Subject Line | Psychology |
|---|---|---|
| A-S1 | Your competitors are pulling ahead on reviews | Threat + specificity. Assumes they're losing, which triggers urgency. |
| A-S2 | Something I noticed about [business type] in [city] | Personal + vague. Creates a curiosity gap they feel compelled to close. |
| A-S3 | How you stack up against other [dentists/plumbers/etc.] in [city] | Direct relevance. Feels like it's specifically about them. |
| A-S4 | The review gap between you and [Competitor] is wider than you think | Highly specific threat. Works best when you can actually name a real competitor. |
| A-S5 | One number that's costing local [business type] owners clients | Problem-framing without revealing the answer. Forces the open. |

**Recommendation:** Test A-S1 and A-S2 first. A-S1 for fear-driven openers, A-S2 for curiosity-driven openers.

> **Subject:** Your competitors are pulling ahead on reviews

I pulled a competitive snapshot for [business type] businesses in [city] — and there's a gap worth paying attention to.

Your top local competitors are adding reviews faster than you are. That kind of momentum compounds: customers almost always choose the business with more reviews, even when the actual quality is identical.

I track this monthly — review growth, market share shifts, customer perception — and I'd like to send you your own free report.

You can request it here (takes 60 seconds): **[website link]**

Craig  
Pulse LCI

---

**Why this works:** Opens with implied bad news (creates tension), explains *why* it matters (review momentum compounds — this is true and specific), then offers the solution. The "60 seconds" framing reduces perceived friction.

## GROUP B — Low-Friction Reply Model

**Goal:** Eliminate website entirely. Test pure demand.  
**Variable being tested:** Does removing the website CTA dramatically increase response rate?  
**Psychology:** Reciprocity + ultra-low friction. Craig is offering to do all the work. The only ask is a reply, which has essentially zero activation energy. The email feels personal and human, not like a mass blast.

---

### Group B — Subject Lines (pick one per send)

| # | Subject Line | Psychology |
|---|---|---|
| B-S1 | Quick question about your competitors | Conversational. Feels like someone who already knows them. |
| B-S2 | Curious — do you track your local competition? | Disarming. Sounds like genuine curiosity, not a pitch. |
| B-S3 | I made something you might find useful | Intriguing. Low-commitment framing — "might" removes pressure. |
| B-S4 | Who are your top 2-3 competitors? | The ask IS the subject line. Extremely direct, feels personal. |
| B-S5 | Free competitive report — no link to click | Directly removes the #1 friction point. Novelty gets opens. |

**Recommendation:** Test B-S1 and B-S4. B-S1 is softer and disarming; B-S4 is unusually direct and will feel jarring in a good way.

---

### Group B — Email Variation 1 (Ultra-Minimal)

> **Subject:** Quick question about your competitors

Hi [First Name],

Do you track how your competitors are doing on reviews each month?

I build monthly competitive intelligence reports for local [business type] owners — review growth, market share shifts, what customers are saying about everyone in the market.

I'd like to put one together for you at no cost. No link to click, no form to fill out.

Just reply with your top 2-3 competitors and I'll send it over.

Craig

---

**Why this works:** Opens with a question (the most human way to start a conversation). The offer is concrete and the ask is the smallest possible: just reply. "No link to click, no form to fill out" directly names and removes the friction. No company branding in the email body — this reads like a person, not a company.

### Group B — Email Variation 3 (Work Already Done)

> **Subject:** Free competitive report — no link to click

Hi [First Name],

I've been building competitive intelligence reports for [business type] businesses — tracking review momentum, local market share, and customer perception month over month.

**Why this works:** The subject line is the pattern interrupt — "no link to click" is so unusual it gets opens. The body delivers on that promise immediately. Saying "I'll build it and send it back" positions Craig as doing the labor, which triggers reciprocity. The prospect feels like they're getting something for almost nothing.

## Experiment Design

### How to run it

Split your next 200 prospects 50/50:
- 100 receive a Group A email (pick one variation + one subject line)
- 100 receive a Group B email (pick one variation + one subject line)

Keep everything else identical: same prospect types, same industries, same send times.

### What to measure

| Metric | Group A | Group B | What it tells you |
|---|---|---|---|
| Open rate | Track via pixel | Track via pixel | Is the subject line working? |
| Click rate | Track via GA / redirect | N/A | Is the email body converting? |
| Website sessions | GA today report | N/A | Is the click reaching the site? |
| Form submissions | Website backend | N/A | Is the site converting? |
| Reply rate | Manual count | Manual count | Is the ask converting? |

### Decision tree after the test

```
Group B replies >> Group A conversions
→ The website is the friction. Fix the landing page or go full reply-first.

Group A clicks but zero form fills
→ The landing page is the leak. Email is working, site isn't.

Both underperform
→ The value proposition isn't landing. Test different industries or framings.

Group A and B perform similarly
→ The barrier isn't the channel. Look at targeting (wrong prospects?).
```

---

## Predictions

**Which gets more replies:** Group B, by a significant margin. Removing the website step eliminates 3 of the 4 friction points in the conversion flow. The ask (reply) is something anyone can do in 15 seconds.

**Which gets more website visits:** Group A, obviously — Group B has no link.

**Which drives more total conversions (any response):** Group B. But the quality of those leads may be lower since the barrier to respond is so low — someone might reply just out of curiosity without serious intent.

**Most likely outcome based on current data:** Group A will continue to show opens with few clicks (the pattern you're already seeing), confirming the email-to-click conversion as the primary leak. Group B will generate replies, which will tell you whether demand actually exists and the website was just in the way.

**The agency owner who replied is a signal:** Agencies responded to a human ask, not a self-serve flow. Group B is essentially the same model applied to business owners directly.

---

## What to do after the test

If Group B wins:
1. Keep the reply-first model for new outreach
2. Redesign the website CTA to feel more like a conversation ("Tell us who your competitors are") rather than a form
3. Consider removing the form entirely and replacing it with a reply-to-email button

If Group A wins:
1. The value proposition is strong enough to drive clicks
2. Optimize the landing page — reduce fields, add social proof, make the CTA feel like a 10-second action
3. Add UTM parameters to every link to confirm which email variant drove each visit
